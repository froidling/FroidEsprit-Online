First-Time Astrophotography, and other updates.

By Ethan | June 15, 2026

Good morning/afternoon/evening folks, please circle as appropriate. It's about time I gave an update on what I've been up to, since it's been a month since I made a long-form post on here, and I only just got started.

To kick things off, the other day, I went out on the field to do some astrophotography. BC has two Dark Sky Parks, areas shielded from light pollution from the big cities for the purposes of stargazing. One is McDonald Park off Highway 1, evenly spaced between Abbotsford and Chilliwack, and the other being Cattle Point on the Oak Bay waterfront. In the wee hours of June 14, I went with the former.

My equipment for the night was my Canon EOS 700D camera - a completely unremarkable 13-year-old DSLR - and a Canon EF 50mm f/1.8 IS STM lens - a completely unremarkable macro lens. For the technically-inclined, I add that because it's a full-frame lens on a crop-sensor camera, my effective focal length was 80mm.

The Scutum star cloud, in the constellation Scutum.

I took about 350 shots while I was out with my father and brother, taking several sets of 15 frames each with a 5-second exposure time. That is to say, in my inexperience, I allowed only 75 seconds of integration time per images. I've begun to get used to learning the workflow, merging the frames together with stars as an anchor in DeepSkyStacker, and piping the resulting output into Lightroom to adjust and pull out the hidden stars. This was by no means professional, and I'm put to shame by the folks at r/Astrophotography, but it's still very good for me as a beginner.

I can't say it was all starshine and roses though. We're a week out from the summer solstice, which I realized only when I was there that astronomical twilight never ends. I was out there past midnight and the sun's light was still peeking through over Sumas Mountain to the north, in addition to the glow of the cities to the east and west. So even at the Dark Sky parksite, it was too bright to see the Milky Way that I was so very anticipating to see.

The Milky Way framing the pale glow of the Cygnus star cloud.

Lessons learned, I know now how to prepare for the next outing.

  1. Use a better lens. I want to get the EF-S 24mm 2.8 IS STM lens, the "Pancake". I'll be able to scope a wider part of the sky, maybe even get the full Milky Way. I also want to try a lens with a greater focal length and see how far I can zoom into the night.

  2. Take more pictures. 75 seconds of integration is nothing. I want to have a full 25 minutes of continuous shooting. A camera sensor is a bucket of light, 15 sets of 5 seconds barely fills it, and I need aggressive processing to expose the hidden details. More light getting through means I can punch through the diffuse gas and see the intricate structures and nebulae.

  3. Different software. DSS is barebones, and Lightroom isn't built for this. I've heard good things about Siril, I want to try that.

  4. Better timing. The ides of June were no good, it was too bright. I feel I'll have better luck mid-July or August, when the twilight ends.
